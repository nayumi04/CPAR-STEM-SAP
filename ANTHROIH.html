Ifugao House

The Ifugao House structures include panolong and hagabi. The panolong, an extended house beam of the torogan (traditional Maranao residence), serves as a symbol of rank and influence. The hagabi functions as a lounging bench made from one tree trunk to symbolize Ifugao social solidarity and prestige since it plays an essential role in their rituals as well as acting as a symbol of wealth. These structures gain cultural value from both their residential functions and their dual roles as exhibits of community ties and societal position and major spiritual elements. Such value demands a deep understanding of their historical and cultural origins. The Ifugao house represents all indigenous cultures and heritages of the Ifugao people as their house is what represents them. The Ifugao house is part of the architectural heritage of the rice terrace landscape which they are also known for because rice terraces are inscribed in the UNESCO World Heritage Sites. Additionally, this was built in honor to give importance to our indigenous ethnolinguistic group in the northern Philippines as their contribution to the country is truly priceless for humanity. The National Museum of Anthropology in Manila maintains the Ifugao house as their newest permanent exhibition. Traditional Cordillera architecture presents itself through bale structures which combine hand-preserved timber with runo and cogon grass. The National Museum recognizes Halipan as one of the key components in this structure because rat guards mounted on each pole contribute to its noteworthy identity. This traditional house first appeared between 1930s through 1940s and moved into the Manila museum in 1998 while requiring restoration efforts. Occupants find daily needs within one room through combined living activities that include storage and cooking and sleeping functions with a removable ladder serving as nightly protection
